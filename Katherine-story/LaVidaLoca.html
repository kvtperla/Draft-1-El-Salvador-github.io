La Vida Loca (2008)

Christian Poveda’s documentary La Vida Local provides us with one of the most influential portrayals of El Salvador lived reality during the 2000s. In this documentary it focuses on the daily lives of MS-13 and Mara 18 members in San Salvador neighbourhoods, displaying the bleak reality of the life of a gang member in El Salvador (Poveda, 2008). Through this film it encodes El Salvador as a country defined by violence, hopelessness, and gang culture (Hall, 1980). For an international audience, the dominant decoding is quite straightforward, El Salvador was an unsafe country as it reinforces its reputation as one of the most violent countries in the world and they take pride in this. However, Salvadorans themselves decode this film differently, recognizing both the accuracy of its depiction and its limitations in representing national identity. Through my perspective, this documentary was shared to me by my parents when I was 9 years old, as a way to display the intense national struggle El Salvador was enduring and its accuracy of displaying the live reality they lived in the past. Which ties into how a Salvadoran will decode this documentary, comparably to an international audience.

Roland Barthes’ concept of mythologies helps explain how La Vida Loca transformed gang violence into a mythic symbol of Salvadoran identity (Barthes 1972). The violence displayed became naturalized as what the country was, overshadowing the other cultural narratives that were being displayed. Nancy Fraser’s critique of the public sphere also applies here, the film amplifies the narrative of gang violence, while silencing the counterpublics who might have emphasized resilience, migration stories, and the communities survival (Fraser, 1990). In this sense, La Vida Loca continues to reinforce the dominant narrative to the public, while dismissing marginalized voices. Karl Marx’s idea of commodity fetishism adds another layer to this. It commodifies the violence that is being consumed by the global audience, amplifying the negative narrative of the country to obscure the social relations of the country's poverty and migration that produced this gang culture within the country (Marx, 1867). The film displays how these forms of organic advertisements in the media marketed the suffering of a country's culture for consumption, continuing to reinforce that negative narrative of the country.
