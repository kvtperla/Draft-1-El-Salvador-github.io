Ruhi Cenet: CECOT Mega-Prison YouTube Documentary (2025)

Perhaps the most striking example of Bukele’s branding strategy is the CECOT mega-prison document, as it showcases a government-supported video of the CECOT prison and the security they have to protect El Salvadorans from gang members (Cenet, 2025). The prison is presented as a proof of safety, encoding El Salvador as a nation that has conquered intense violence and gang violence (Hall, 1980). International audiences will often decode this as evidence of progression and prosperity, while Salvadorans will criticize this form of advertisement as a propaganda masking the repression in the country.

The prison becomes the mythic symbol of order and change, it forms itself as a timeless feature of Salvadoran identity and future (Barthes, 1972). Yet, the advertisement continues to erase the historical struggle that Salvadorans endured prior and only displays the safety of the country today. The counterpublics narratives are silenced through advertisements like these, particularly those concerned with human rights being abused and authoritarianism (Fraser, 1990). Which is the lived reality of many Salvadorans today who live in fear of being accused as a gang-member and ending up in a prison like the CECOT. The Bukele administration commodified this idea of safety to his viewers, to market El Salvador as a country of progression from its past (Marx, 1867). The country itself then becomes a product not only for tourists, but for international investors because of its publicized progression of becoming the safest country in only 5 years. Through advertisements like CECOT Mega-Prison, it displays how a state repression can be commodified for consumers.
