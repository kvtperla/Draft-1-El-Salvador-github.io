El Salvador Past Tourism Efforts

Vanegas examines this relationship between tourism and economic growth in El Salvador, tourism has potential to stimulate this development, but has been undermined through weak branding and insecurity (Vanegas, 2012). He views tourism as a fragile sector, this is why El Salvador heavily depends on its tourism effort for safety and stability. This effort of prompting this safety and stability comes from its historical reality, acknowledging the past is what gains traction and interest for El Salvador’s tourism industry. Guaita Martinez et al. expands this idea by analyzing tourism competitiveness across Latin America (Guaita Martinez et al, 2022). They argue countries with strong branding strategies and infrastructure investments have a likely chance to attract international visitors. Compared to El Salvador, in the past, it ranked low in competitiveness during the early 2010s which reinforced the negative image and was overshadowed by violence within the country. Through their analysis, it displays how branding and perception are a crucial focus in creating natural attraction to a country and shaping tourism outcomes.

Patel focuses on forms of coastal tourism development, highlighting the uneven distribution of resources and opportunities along Latin America’s Pacific coast (Patel, 2023). In El Salvador, Patel notes that surf tourism was a niche attraction but lacked government support and infrastructure (Patel, 2023). Which coincides with Latarola’s study of surf tourism, which emphasizes how local communities will market El Salvador’s beaches informally, long before Bukele’s administration formalized “Surf City” (Latarola, 2013). Which displays how coastal tourism was promoted internally through the invisible work by the community, but remained a missed opportunity from the country to profit from in the past. Reiser et al. builds on this by turning to community-based tourism, arguing that community involvement is essential for sustainable development (Reiser et al., 2025). Through their findings, when communities are involved with tourism efforts, the benefits extend beyond economic growth but also include cultural preservation and social empowerment (Reiser et al., 2025). With El Salvador, this shifts the question on whether Bukele’s top-down branding strategy is truly engaging with local communities or simply commodifying its national identity for global consumption.

In Paula and Eloy study of Mexico’s tourism branding, they provide a comparative lens by arguing a national identity is often commodified through selective images to appeal to an international tourist (Paula and Eloy, 2013). Which is related to El Salvador's framework, where campaigns like El Salvador Great Like Our People and Surf City, both highlight beaches and hospitality but downplay the poverty, migration, and violence within the country (El Salvador Marca Pais, 2017 and Nayib Bukele, 2025). These advertisement efforts help El Salvador’s branding in a broader perspective, and follows patterns of Latin American tourism, where identity is commodified for tourists. Gutierrez critiques these tourism efforts, by highlighting these functions as a form of resource extractions, reshaping cities and landscapes to serve global markets rather than focusing on local needs (Gutierrez, 2023). Which helps analyze Bukele’s campaigns that prioritizes international perception and investment while raising concerns about the live reality that isn’t being glorified online
