Surf City (2019–present)

ABukele’s Surf city initiative represents a more aggressive and globally visible rebranding strategy for the country (Nayib Bukele, 2025). In previous years, El Salvador had very few tourist locations which made it difficult for the government to market itself online. Through the investment of coastal infrastructures and promoting surfing as a global attraction, Bukele administration was able to encode a new perspective of El Salvador (Hall, 1980). Displaying the country as a modern, adventurous, and safe country to visit, that held national events for international visitors (Nayib Bukele, 2025). Through an international tourist perspective, they will decode Surf City as an example of transformation, while Salvadorans will interpret this as selective storytelling that commodifies the beaches but ignoring the inequalities that lie underneath them.

Barther’s mythologies help explain how surfing is presented as a timeless symbol of Salvadoran identity (Barthes, 1972). This myth erases the historical reality that tourism had long been undermined by violence. Surf City constructs a dominant narrative of prosperity while silencing counterpublics concerns with displacement or its environmental impact within the local city (Fraser, 1990). Using Terranova's free labour framework is especially relevant here. As Surf City heavily relies on digital promotion, with Salvadorans and tourist posting images and videos amplifies the campaign without compensation (Terranova, 2000). Their unpaid cultural labour blurs the line between government campaigns versus organic enthusiasm. It displays how surfing itself becomes commodified for the user and the consumer, as they continue to promote this throughout media platforms which enhances this new identity of El Salvador.
